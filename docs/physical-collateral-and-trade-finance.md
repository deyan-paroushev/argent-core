# Physical Collateral and the Financing of Trade

**A primer on where credit against physical assets sits in the financial system, who it touches, how it is coordinated, and where the category is heading.**

*This document is background reading, not investment, legal, or financial advice. It surveys a field far larger than any single product. Figures are drawn from public sources and cited inline; where estimates vary between sources, that variation is stated rather than resolved. A short closing section notes where Argent fits, clearly separated from the neutral survey that precedes it.*

---

## 1. Why physical collateral matters

Most credit in the world is secured. A lender advances money and takes, in return, a claim on something the borrower owns, so that if the borrower cannot repay, the lender has a path to recovery. That something is collateral. When it is a building or a financial security, the arrangement is well understood and heavily systematised. When it is a physical, movable thing, a stack of copper cathodes in a warehouse, a tank of fuel, a silo of grain, gold bars in a vault, the arrangement is older, larger, and in important respects less legible than the systems built around real estate and securities.

This matters because physical goods are how a large part of the real economy stores and moves value. A commodity trader's wealth is in metal in transit. A producer's working capital is locked in unsold inventory. A refiner's balance sheet is bars awaiting sale. For all of these, the ability to borrow against the physical thing, rather than selling it or waiting for it to convert to cash, is what keeps the business moving. Secured credit against physical assets is, in a real sense, the circulatory system of physical trade.

The basic distinction that runs through this whole topic is between the **asset** and the **control over the asset**. Two lenders can look at the same tonne of copper and see entirely different things: one sees a valuable object, the other sees a set of unanswered questions, what exactly is it, where is it, who else has a claim, at what value, who may release it, and what happens on default. The object is the easy part. The legibility and control of the object is the hard part, and it is where most of the cost and most of the friction in this field actually live.

## 2. What is measured, and what is not measured

Before any figures, an honest caveat. The precise thing this primer is about, credit extended against physical assets held in custody, for operational or working-capital purposes, is **not** cleanly measured as a single category by anyone. It does not appear as a line item in official statistics, because it sits at the intersection of several separately-tracked categories: commodity trade finance, inventory and warehouse-receipt finance, and asset-based lending. No standard source isolates the intersection.

This is not a gap in the research; it is a feature of how the field is tracked, and it is itself informative. The reason the category is hard to measure is the same reason it is underserved: it is fragmented, documentary, and spread across parties and systems that do not report to a common frame. Even the basic data on trade finance is acknowledged to be incomplete. The Asian Development Bank, whose survey is the leading benchmark in the field, notes that a comprehensive, systematic collection of trade-credit data has been recommended by the G20 and coordinated among international bodies precisely because it does not yet fully exist.[^adb2025]

What follows, therefore, is a set of strong surrounding anchors, not a single clean market size. Anyone who cites a single confident number for this exact category is estimating past what the data supports.

## 3. The strongest numerical anchors

### 3.1 Trade finance gap: the macro access problem

The clearest measure of the *consequence* of coordination failing is unmet demand. The ADB estimates the global trade finance gap, the unmet demand from companies for financing to support the movement of goods, at roughly US$2.5 trillion in 2025, about 10% of global trade, essentially unchanged since 2023.[^adb2025] The gap falls hardest on smaller firms, though recent data suggests SME rejection rates have narrowed toward those of larger corporates.[^adb2025] Persistent among the reported obstacles are compliance and due-diligence burdens and the difficulty of establishing trust in the underlying transaction. A meaningful share of this gap is not a shortage of capital but a shortage of the confidence and legibility that would let capital be deployed against real goods.

### 3.2 Commodity finance: the closest large-market proxy

Commodity trade finance, the financing of physical supply chains in metals, energy, and agriculture, is reputed to account for more than US$3 trillion in lending.[^afme] It is the closest large, named market to the topic of this primer, and its mechanics (borrowing bases, advance rates, overcollateralization, first-priority claims over financed goods) are exactly those of physical-collateral credit.[^afme] It is a proxy, not an identity: it includes receivables and prepayment structures that are not physical-in-custody, so it should be cited as the surrounding market, not as the addressable one.

### 3.3 Corporate collateral: the ECB evidence for why collateral matters

A study of the euro-area corporate credit register (AnaCredit) found that roughly 70% of credit amounts are collateralized, and that collateralized loans carry materially better terms: 33% to 48% larger committed amounts and 10 to 18 basis points lower interest rates than comparable unsecured loans.[^ecb3095] Crucially for this topic, the same study found that physical movable assets are among the *least*-pledged forms of collateral, well behind real estate and financial assets.[^ecb3095] Physical goods secure a great deal of credit in absolute terms, yet relative to their economic weight they are underused as collateral. That gap is the central tension of the field.

### 3.4 Warehouse receipts: how stored goods become collateral

Where goods sit in a certified warehouse, a receipt evidencing their quantity and quality can serve as security for a loan, letting a producer borrow against stored crops or metal rather than selling immediately.[^worldbank2025] Advances are typically a discounted fraction of value, commonly 50% to 80% depending on the commodity and the robustness of the system.[^worldbank2017] The reliability of the whole structure depends on the reliability of the underlying infrastructure: the warehouse's standards of inspection, storage, and record-keeping, and the legal enforceability of the lender's rights.[^worldbank2025]

### 3.5 Gold: the first mature physical collateral adapter

Gold is a useful reference because it is liquid, standardized, and deeply institutionally held. Its over-the-counter market trades on the order of US$120 billion per day, with total daily volume including futures materially higher.[^suerf][^wgcvolumes] Central banks have purchased over 1,000 tonnes of gold annually for several consecutive years, an unprecedented sustained accumulation.[^wgccb] These facts make gold the clearest worked example of a custody-stable physical asset used at institutional scale. The precise regulatory framing is addressed in section 6.4, because it is an area where misinformation is common and precision matters.

## 4. Who is affected: the parties and their vantage points

Credit against physical collateral is not a two-party affair. It is a coordination problem among several parties, each of whom sees the same transaction differently and carries a different risk. A change that helps one party can complicate matters for another, which is a large part of why the field changes slowly.

**The borrower (trader, producer, processor, SME).** The borrower owns the goods and needs the cash. Their working capital is frozen in pledged inventory they cannot rotate, sell, or partially reclaim without unwinding the whole arrangement. For a business whose goods turn over in days, rigid collateral is a direct operational cost. The borrower wants flexibility: to substitute one batch for another, to sell part of the stock and release the corresponding portion of the pledge, to keep operating while borrowing.

**The bank or lender.** The lender advances the money and bears the credit risk. What it needs is confidence in the collateral: that it exists, that it is the stated quantity and quality, that no one else has a claim on it, that its value covers the loan with a margin, and that if things go wrong the claim can be enforced. The lender's recurring difficulty is not the concept but the operation. Industry analysis consistently finds collateral operations running on manual, spreadsheet-bound, fragmented processes, with real risks of improper release of collateral and misreporting of collateral values, most exposed during volatility when workloads spike and errors compound.[^boe] The lender wants control and legibility, and often has neither in real time. It compensates for that lack with conservative advance rates, extra documentation, restricted release, or outright refusal.

**The custodian, warehouse operator, or collateral manager.** This party physically holds the goods and attests to their state. In much of commodity finance the lender never takes possession; instead it relies on a third party who acknowledges the lender's interest and holds the goods on its behalf. This is the legal mechanism of *constructive possession*, achieved through an arrangement by which the custodian recognises the lender's claim and issues evidence, such as a warehouse receipt, of that interest. The custodian's burden is reconciliation and dispute: they sign for the state of the goods, but the record of who authorised what, and when, is often fragmented across systems and paper. They benefit from anything that makes their attestations cleaner and reduces their liability.

**The insurer and the underwriter.** Physical goods in storage and transit are insured, and the insurer carries exposure it cannot always see clearly. Loss, damage, spoilage, and misappropriation are real risks in commodity collateral, which is one reason lenders enter collateral-management agreements to ensure goods are stored safely and not commingled, spoiled, or stolen. The clearer the control and custody record, the better the insurer can price and manage that exposure.

**The auditor, and behind the auditor, the supervisor.** After the fact, someone must reconstruct what happened: what was pledged, who approved its release, whether coverage held, whether enforcement was valid. Today this reconstruction is often painful, assembled from disparate systems and documents. Supervisors increasingly expect collateral evidence to be auditable and current rather than reconstructed on demand, part of a broader regulatory push toward more legible, less manual collateral operations.[^boe]

The important observation is that these parties are not simply aligned customers of one service. Their incentives overlap but also pull against each other. The borrower wants flexibility; the lender wants control; the custodian wants to limit liability; the insurer wants visibility; the supervisor wants auditability. Any improvement to the field has to satisfy several of these at once, which is precisely what makes the field slow to change.

## 5. Coordination: what can and cannot be measured

The theme running through the parties and the mechanics is coordination. A financed physical asset is a shared state that several parties must agree on: it exists, it is worth this much, it is pledged to this lender, it may be released under these conditions, it has now been sold and the proceeds applied. Each party holds part of this truth, and the arrangement works only when their views are kept consistent.

There is no single accepted statistic called "coordination cost between parties in trade finance," and that absence is itself worth stating. Coordination is observed only indirectly, through its symptoms: the trade finance gap, rejected applications, document discrepancies, settlement delays, collateral-release delays, disputes over title or custody or quantity, operational losses and fraud, the time from a real-world event to lender-readable evidence, and the degree of overcollateralization a lender demands because it lacks confidence.

At the operational level, coordination failure can be described as a mismatch between five records that are supposed to describe the same asset:

```text
borrower record
bank record
custodian / warehouse record
legal / document record
settlement / payment record
```

The harder the asset is to verify and control, the more these records drift apart, and the more the lender compensates through conservative advance rates, manual checks, additional documentation, restricted release, or refusal to finance. A useful analytical way to state the relationship:

> The cost of physical collateral finance rises with the number of parties that must agree on the same asset state, the number of documents required to prove that state, and the time lag between real-world events and lender-readable evidence.

This is not a formal regulatory metric. It is an analytical lens, and it should be presented as one. But it explains, better than any single number, why physical collateral can be genuinely valuable and still be underused: the value is in the object, and the cost is in reconciling everyone's view of it.

## 6. What is changing now

Several forces are reshaping this field at once. They point in a consistent direction without guaranteeing any particular pace or outcome.

### 6.1 Collateral is being treated as infrastructure

Regulation and market infrastructure are converging on the idea that collateral control is itself a system worth modernising, not a back-office afterthought. The finalisation of the Basel III framework presses banks toward more efficient collateral strategies and more active management of risk-weighted assets. In parallel, the Bank of England has identified the difficulty of mobilising and substituting collateral quickly as a core inefficiency, and pointed to programmability as part of the remedy.[^boe] The Eurosystem's Appia roadmap and its Pontes initiative are building toward settlement in central-bank money for DLT-based transactions.[^ecbappia] The current runs toward collateral that can be seen, moved, and substituted cleanly.

### 6.2 Asset-based finance is expanding, but categories remain blurred

Lending secured against tangible assets is a large and growing category. Estimates of its size vary considerably between commercial research providers, with 2025 figures ranging from roughly US$400 billion to US$900 billion globally depending on scope and definition.[^abl1][^abl2] The sources agree on direction, double-digit growth driven by demand for working capital and flexible collateral-based financing, but the wide spread between them is itself a caution: the category is bounded differently by different analysts, and no single figure should be cited as authoritative.

### 6.3 Digitization is attacking documents before it attacks collateral control

Much of the digitization in trade finance so far has targeted the *documentary* layer, electronic bills of lading, digital letters of credit, standardised messaging, rather than the *control* layer, the live state of who may do what with the collateral. Within asset-based lending, a large majority of lenders now use digital tools to value collateral, and "technology-enabled collateral controls" are cited as a growth driver, while on-chain collateral tracking remains used by only a small minority of lenders, mostly for multi-jurisdictional or syndicated deals. The documentary front is maturing; the control front is earlier.

### 6.4 Gold is becoming more important, but the claim must stay precise

Gold is a space where online misinformation is common, so precision is essential to credibility. The accurate framing is: gold has deep trading volume and mature market infrastructure; central-bank demand has been historically strong; there is an active policy debate about gold's treatment as a high-quality liquid asset; and gold is **not** currently classified as an HQLA under Basel III liquidity rules, with no announced change.[^wgchqla] A separate and often-confused fact is that allocated physical gold has carried a 0% risk weight for capital purposes since Basel I, which is a capital rule, not a liquidity (HQLA) classification. Any credible document in this space must keep these two distinct, and must not repeat the common online claim that gold became a Tier 1 HQLA in 2025. It did not.

## 7. Where the category is heading, honestly

The forces above suggest a direction, but a balanced view holds the tailwinds and the headwinds together.

The tailwinds are real. A persistent multi-trillion-dollar financing gap creates pressure to finance more of the real economy's physical value. Regulation rewards efficient, legible collateral. The operational layer is digitising. Physical assets are gaining financial standing. Market infrastructures are actively building toward programmable settlement and collateral mobility. Taken together, these favour collateral that is more legible, more controllable, and more mobile.

The headwinds deserve equal weight. This is a conservative, heavily regulated field that changes slowly by design; the parties carry long memories of losses and are rightly cautious. The coordination problem is genuinely hard precisely because it involves several parties with partly conflicting incentives, and any solution must satisfy lender, borrower, custodian, insurer, and supervisor at once. Legal enforceability of collateral rights varies by jurisdiction and remains a real constraint, especially cross-border. New models, including on-chain approaches to collateral control, remain largely unproven at institutional scale, and the burden of proof sits with them. And the same fragmentation that creates the opportunity also raises the switching cost: incumbent processes, however manual, are embedded in contracts, systems, and habits.

The honest synthesis is that the direction of travel favours more legible, controllable, mobile physical collateral, but the pace is uncertain and gated on trust, proof, and coordination rather than on the availability of technology. The technology to make collateral control legible largely exists; whether and how fast the field adopts it depends on whether several parties can be brought to rely on a shared record together. This is a coordination transition, and coordination transitions are slow, uneven, and easy to overpredict.

## 8. Claims to avoid

Because this field attracts overstatement, a short discipline is worth stating plainly. A credible document in this space should **not** claim:

- that a chain or database verifies physical truth (it records attestations, not ground truth);
- that gold is currently a Basel III HQLA (it is not);
- that the addressable market is the entire commodity-finance or trade-finance figure (those are surrounding markets, not the addressable one);
- that tokenization alone solves physical-collateral control;
- that release or enforcement can happen automatically in the physical world (a custodian still performs the physical act);
- that physical collateral can be rehypothecated like liquid financial collateral without legal and custody controls;
- that any single platform can replace custodians, banks, legal agreements, or courts.

Safer, more defensible language: the record captures signed attestations and control events; the contract enforces the agreed control logic inside that record; custodians and legal documents remain the source of physical and legal truth; and the opportunity is a process gap inside physical-collateral finance, not the whole of trade finance.

## 9. Where Argent fits

*This section is included for completeness and is clearly separated from the neutral survey above.*

Argent is one specific response to the coordination problem described in this primer. Its position is narrow and deliberate: the physical asset stays in professional custody and is never tokenized; what becomes legible is the *control* over it, recorded as a sequence of signed, auditable events, who attested to the goods, who pledged them, who may release them, under what conditions, and whether the lender's coverage still holds at each step. In the terms of section 5, Argent's aim is to reduce the drift between the five records by making the control state a single signed record the parties share, rather than a reconciliation each performs separately.

Where much recent work on real-world assets focuses on tokenizing ownership so an asset can trade, Argent addresses the different and, it argues, more common institutional need: not to move title, but to make control over a physical asset legible and governable while ownership and the asset stay exactly where they are. It does not attempt to resolve the parts of the problem that lie outside a control record. It does not verify physical truth, replace the custodian's role, enforce legal rights, or determine a lender's risk model; those remain with custodians, courts, and the institutions themselves.

Whether that approach proves useful at scale is exactly the kind of open question this primer's earlier sections frame. It is a coordination bet, and like all such bets it will be settled by adoption and proof rather than by argument. The technical design, boundaries, and current status are documented separately in `protocol.md`, `argent-architecture.md`, and `collateral-control.md`.
